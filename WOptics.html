Comprehensive notes based on NCERT Class 12 Physics - Chapter 10

1. Introduction

Wave Theory: Proposed by Christiaan Huygens in 1678. It predicts that light behaves as a wave. This theory contradicted Newton's Corpuscular Model (particle nature) but was later confirmed by experiments like interference (Thomas Young, 1801) and diffraction.

Maxwell's Contribution: Predicted light as electromagnetic waves, not requiring a medium to propagate.

2. Huygens Principle

Huygens' Principle is a geometric construction to find the shape of a wavefront at a later time.

  • Wavefront: The locus of all points oscillating in the same phase. (e.g., Spherical wavefront from a point source, Plane wavefront from a distant source).
  • Secondary Wavelets: Every point on a wavefront acts as a source of secondary spherical wavelets spreading out at the speed of light.
  • New Wavefront: The envelope (tangent surface) of these secondary wavelets at a later time gives the new wavefront.
Figure 10.1 (Animated): Expanding spherical wavefronts from a point source according to Huygens' principle.

3. Refraction and Reflection (Wave Theory)

Refraction

Using Huygens' principle, we can derive Snell's Law. If \(v_1\) and \(v_2\) are speeds of light in medium 1 and 2:

$$ \frac{\sin i}{\sin r} = \frac{v_1}{v_2} = \frac{n_2}{n_1} $$
  • Key Result: If light bends towards the normal (\(r < i\)), then \(v_2 < v_1\). Light travels slower in a denser medium (confirming wave theory over particle theory).
  • Frequency: Remains constant during refraction.
  • Wavelength: Changes. \(\lambda_2 = \frac{v_2}{v_1} \lambda_1\).

Doppler Effect

Change in frequency due to motion of source/observer. $$ \frac{\Delta \nu}{\nu} = -\frac{v_{radial}}{c} $$ Red Shift: Source moves away (Wavelength increases). Blue Shift: Source moves closer.

4. Interference of Light

The phenomenon of redistribution of light energy due to the superposition of light waves from two coherent sources.

Coherent Sources

Sources that maintain a constant phase difference. Two independent sources (e.g., two bulbs) are incoherent and cannot produce sustained interference.

Young's Double Slit Experiment (YDSE)

Thomas Young (1801) demonstrated interference using two slits illuminated by a single source.

Figure 10.2 (Animated): Waves from two slits overlap to form bright (constructive) and dark (destructive) fringes on the screen.

Conditions for Constructive Interference (Bright Fringe)

Path difference is an integral multiple of wavelength.

$$ S_2P - S_1P = n\lambda \quad (n=0, 1, 2...) $$

Conditions for Destructive Interference (Dark Fringe)

Path difference is an odd integral multiple of half-wavelength.

$$ S_2P - S_1P = (n + \frac{1}{2})\lambda \quad (n=0, 1, 2...) $$

Fringe Width (\(\beta\))

$$ \beta = \frac{\lambda D}{d} $$

Where \(D\) is screen distance, and \(d\) is slit separation.

5. Diffraction

The bending of light around the corners of an obstacle or aperture. It is a general characteristic of waves.

Single Slit Diffraction

When light passes through a single slit of width \(a\), a central bright maximum is formed, surrounded by alternating dark and bright bands.

Figure 10.3 (Animated): Intensity distribution of single-slit diffraction showing a broad central maximum.

Condition for Minima (Dark Fringes):

$$ a \sin \theta = n\lambda \quad (n = 1, 2, 3...) $$

Note: The central maximum width is approximately \(2\lambda D / a\).

Resolving Power

Telescope: Angular resolution \(\Delta \theta = 0.61 \lambda / a\).
Microscope: Minimum separation \(d_{min} = 1.22 \lambda / (2 n \sin \beta)\).

Validity of Ray Optics

Ray optics is valid when the size of the aperture is large compared to wavelength. The Fresnel Distance (\(Z_F\)) is the distance at which diffraction spread equals slit size:

$$ Z_F = \frac{a^2}{\lambda} $$

6. Polarisation

Light is a Transverse Wave. The electric field vector oscillates perpendicular to the direction of propagation. In unpolarised light, these vibrations are in all random directions in the transverse plane.

Figure 10.4 (Animated): Unpolarised light passes through a polaroid and becomes Linearly Polarised (vibrations restricted to one plane).

Malus' Law

When plane polarised light passes through an analyzer oriented at angle \(\theta\) to the polarizer, the transmitted intensity \(I\) is:

$$ I = I_0 \cos^2 \theta $$

Polarisation by Reflection (Brewster's Law)

At a specific angle of incidence (\(i_B\)), the reflected light is completely polarised perpendicular to the plane of incidence.

$$ \tan i_B = n $$

Where \(n\) is the refractive index of the medium.
