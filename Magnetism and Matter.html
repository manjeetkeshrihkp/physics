1. The Bar Magnet

A bar magnet behaves like a Magnetic Dipole. It has two poles, North (N) and South (S), of equal strength. Monopoles do not exist; cutting a magnet gives two smaller magnets.

Figure 5.1: Magnetic field lines form continuous closed loops, moving N to S outside and S to N inside.

Magnetic Field Lines

  • They form continuous closed loops.
  • Tangent at any point gives the direction of the magnetic field \( \mathbf{B} \).
  • They never intersect.

Dipole in Uniform Magnetic Field

A bar magnet of moment \( \mathbf{m} \) placed in a uniform field \( \mathbf{B} \) experiences a torque:

$$ \tau = \mathbf{m} \times \mathbf{B} = mB \sin\theta $$ $$ U = -\mathbf{m} \cdot \mathbf{B} = -mB \cos\theta $$

Time period of oscillation: \( T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{I}{mB}} \).

2. Magnetism and Gauss’s Law

Gauss’s law for magnetism states that the net magnetic flux through any closed surface is zero. This confirms that isolated magnetic monopoles do not exist.

$$ \Phi_B = \oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = 0 $$

3. The Earth’s Magnetism

The Earth acts like a giant magnetic dipole. Its magnetic North pole is near the geographic South pole, and vice versa.

~11.3° Tilt
Figure 5.2: Earth's magnetic axis is tilted by ~11.3° relative to the geographic rotation axis.

Elements of Earth’s Magnetic Field

  • Magnetic Declination (D): Angle between the geographic meridian and the magnetic meridian.
  • Angle of Dip or Inclination (I): Angle that the total magnetic field \( \mathbf{B}_E \) makes with the horizontal.
  • Horizontal Component (\( H_E \)): \( H_E = B_E \cos I \).
$$ \tan I = \frac{Z_E}{H_E} = \frac{B_E \sin I}{B_E \cos I} $$

4. Magnetization & Magnetic Intensity

When a material is placed in a magnetic field, it gets magnetized.

  • Magnetization (\( \mathbf{M} \)): Net magnetic moment per unit volume. \( \mathbf{M} = \mathbf{m}_{net} / V \). Unit: A/m.
  • Magnetic Intensity (\( \mathbf{H} \)): Defined as \( \mathbf{H} = \frac{\mathbf{B}}{\mu_0} - \mathbf{M} \).
  • Susceptibility (\( \chi \)): \( \mathbf{M} = \chi \mathbf{H} \).
  • Relative Permeability (\( \mu_r \)): \( \mu_r = 1 + \chi \).
$$ \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 (\mathbf{H} + \mathbf{M}) = \mu_0 \mu_r \mathbf{H} = \mu \mathbf{H} $$

Curie's Law

For paramagnetic materials, susceptibility is inversely proportional to temperature: \( \chi = C/T \).

For ferromagnets, above the Curie Temperature (\( T_c \)), they become paramagnetic.
