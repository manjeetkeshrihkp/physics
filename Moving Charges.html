Moving Charges & Magnetism

Physics • Class 12 • Chapter 4

Detailed Physics Notes

Comprehensive notes based on NCERT Class 12 Physics - Chapter 4

1. Introduction & Oersted's Experiment

Connection between Electricity and Magnetism: Discovered by Hans Christian Oersted in 1820. He observed that a magnetic compass needle deflected when placed near a wire carrying an electric current.

  • Moving charges or currents produce a magnetic field in the surrounding space.
  • The direction of the magnetic field depends on the direction of the current (Right-Hand Rule).

2. Magnetic Force (Lorentz Force)

A charge \( q \) moving with velocity \( \mathbf{v} \) in the presence of both electric field \( \mathbf{E} \) and magnetic field \( \mathbf{B} \) experiences a force called the Lorentz Force.

$$ \mathbf{F} = q[\mathbf{E} + (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B})] $$

Magnetic Force Features

  • Depends on \( q, \mathbf{v} \) and \( \mathbf{B} \).
  • Force is zero if velocity is parallel or anti-parallel to the magnetic field.
  • Force acts perpendicular to both velocity and the magnetic field.
  • No Work Done: Since magnetic force is perpendicular to velocity, no work is done by the magnetic field on the charge. Kinetic energy remains constant; only direction changes.

Unit of B: Tesla (T). 1 Gauss = \( 10^{-4} \) Tesla.

3. Motion in a Magnetic Field

Circular Motion

If velocity \( \mathbf{v} \) is perpendicular to \( \mathbf{B} \), the particle describes a circle.

×
Figure 4.1: Charged particle executing circular motion in a uniform magnetic field (perpendicular).
$$ r = \frac{mv}{qB} $$ $$ \omega = 2\pi \nu = \frac{qB}{m} \quad \text{(Cyclotron Frequency)} $$

Note: Frequency is independent of speed and radius.

Helical Motion

If velocity has a component parallel to \( \mathbf{B} \), the path is a helix.

Figure 4.2: Helical path of a charged particle when velocity is not perpendicular to the magnetic field.

Pitch (p): Distance moved along the field in one rotation: \( p = v_{||} T = \frac{2\pi m v_{||}}{qB} \).

4. Biot-Savart Law

Gives the magnetic field \( d\mathbf{B} \) due to a small current element \( I d\mathbf{l} \).

$$ d\mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \frac{I d\mathbf{l} \times \mathbf{r}}{r^3} $$
  • \( \mu_0 \): Permeability of free space = \( 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{Tm/A} \).
  • Direction is given by the Right-Hand Screw Rule.

5. Ampere's Circuital Law

The line integral of magnetic field around a closed loop is equal to \( \mu_0 \) times the total current threading the loop.

$$ \oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I $$

Field due to Long Straight Wire

$$ B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r} $$

Field lines are concentric circles around the wire.

6. The Solenoid

A long wire wound in a close-packed helix. The field inside a long solenoid is uniform and strong.

Figure 4.3: Magnetic field lines inside a solenoid are parallel (uniform).
$$ B = \mu_0 n I $$

Where \( n \) is the number of turns per unit length. The field outside is approximately zero.

7. Force between Two Parallel Currents

Two parallel conductors carrying current exert magnetic forces on each other.

↑ I1
↑ I2
Figure 4.4: Parallel currents attract each other. Anti-parallel currents repel.
$$ F = \frac{\mu_0 I_1 I_2}{2\pi d} L $$

Force per unit length: \( f = \frac{\mu_0 I_1 I_2}{2\pi d} \).

Definition of Ampere

One Ampere is the constant current which, if maintained in two straight parallel conductors of infinite length, placed 1 meter apart in vacuum, would produce a force of \( 2 \times 10^{-7} \) N/m.

8. Torque on Current Loop (Magnetic Dipole)

A rectangular loop carrying current in a uniform magnetic field experiences a torque (but no net force).

$$ \tau = \mathbf{m} \times \mathbf{B} = m B \sin\theta $$

Where \( \mathbf{m} = NIA \) is the Magnetic Dipole Moment (N turns, current I, Area A). The loop behaves like a magnetic dipole.

9. The Moving Coil Galvanometer

A device to detect and measure currents. It uses the principle that a current-carrying coil in a magnetic field experiences a torque.

  • Radial Magnetic Field: Used to ensure torque is constant and maximum.
  • Equilibrium: Magnetic Torque = Restoring Spring Torque (\( k\phi \)).
    $$ \phi = \left( \frac{NAB}{k} \right) I $$

Conversions

  • To Ammeter: Connect a small resistance (shunt, \( r_s \)) in parallel.
  • To Voltmeter: Connect a large resistance (\( R \)) in series.
