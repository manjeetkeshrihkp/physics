1. Introduction

In previous chapters, we learned that an electric current produces a magnetic field and a changing magnetic field produces an electric field. James Clerk Maxwell (1865) argued that the converse must also be true: a time-varying electric field must produce a magnetic field.

This hypothesis led to the prediction of Electromagnetic (EM) Waves, which are coupled time-varying electric and magnetic fields propagating through space.

2. Displacement Current

Maxwell noticed an inconsistency in Ampere's Circuital Law when applied to a charging capacitor. To resolve this, he introduced the concept of Displacement Current (\(I_d\)).

Figure 8.1: A changing Electric Field (red lines) between capacitor plates induces a Magnetic Field (blue ring), just like a conduction current would.

The displacement current is given by:

$$ I_d = \epsilon_0 \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt} $$

Where \(\Phi_E\) is the electric flux. The total current is the sum of conduction current (\(I_c\)) and displacement current (\(I_d\)). Ampere's Law is modified to the Ampere-Maxwell Law:

$$ \oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 (I_c + I_d) = \mu_0 I_c + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt} $$

3. Maxwell's Equations

Maxwell unified electricity and magnetism into four fundamental equations. These equations govern all electromagnetic phenomena.

  1. Gauss's Law for Electricity:
    $$ \oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \frac{Q}{\epsilon_0} $$
  2. Gauss's Law for Magnetism:
    $$ \oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = 0 $$
    (Implies no magnetic monopoles exist).
  3. Faraday's Law:
    $$ \oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = - \frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} $$
  4. Ampere-Maxwell Law:
    $$ \oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I_c + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt} $$

4. Electromagnetic Waves

Nature of EM Waves

EM waves are transverse waves consisting of oscillating electric (\(\mathbf{E}\)) and magnetic (\(\mathbf{B}\)) fields.

  • \(\mathbf{E}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\) are perpendicular to each other.
  • Both are perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation.
Figure 8.2: An EM wave propagating to the right. Red represents the Electric Field (Y-axis), Blue represents the Magnetic Field (Z-axis). They oscillate in phase.

Properties

  • Speed: In vacuum, they travel at the speed of light (\(c\)).
    $$ c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}} \approx 3 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s} $$
  • Relation between E and B: The amplitudes are related by \(E_0 = c B_0\).
  • Energy Density: Energy is shared equally between electric and magnetic fields. Total energy density \(u\):
    $$ u_{avg} = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 E_{rms}^2 + \frac{1}{2\mu_0} B_{rms}^2 $$
  • Momentum: EM waves carry momentum (\(p = U/c\)) and exert radiation pressure.
