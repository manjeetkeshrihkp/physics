1. Introduction

Semiconductors are materials whose conductivity lies between conductors (metals) and insulators. They are the foundation of modern electronics. Unlike vacuum tubes, semiconductor devices (diodes, transistors) are small, consume low power, operate at low voltage, and have a long life.

Classification based on Conductivity (\(\sigma\))

  • Metals: Very low resistivity (\(\rho \sim 10^{-2} - 10^{-8} \, \Omega \text{m}\)).
  • Semiconductors: Intermediate resistivity (\(\rho \sim 10^{-5} - 10^{6} \, \Omega \text{m}\)).
  • Insulators: High resistivity (\(\rho \sim 10^{11} - 10^{19} \, \Omega \text{m}\)).

2. Energy Bands in Solids

In a solid, electrons are arranged in energy bands separated by an Energy Gap (\(E_g\)).

  • Valence Band (VB): Contains valence electrons.
  • Conduction Band (CB): Contains free electrons responsible for conduction.
  • Energy Gap (\(E_g\)): The energy difference between the top of VB and bottom of CB.
Conduction Band (Empty at 0K)
Valence Band (Filled)
Thermal excitation causes an electron to jump from VB to CB, leaving behind a hole (vacancy).

Classification based on Band Gap (\(E_g\))

  • Metals: \(E_g \approx 0\). CB and VB overlap. Electrons are free to move.
  • Semiconductors: \(E_g < 3\) eV. Small gap (e.g., Si = 1.1 eV, Ge = 0.7 eV). Thermal energy can excite electrons.
  • Insulators: \(E_g > 3\) eV. Large gap. No conduction.

3. Intrinsic Semiconductor

Pure semiconductors (like pure Si or Ge) are called Intrinsic Semiconductors.

  • At \(T > 0\)K, thermal energy generates electron-hole pairs.
  • Number of free electrons (\(n_e\)) = Number of holes (\(n_h\)) = Intrinsic carrier concentration (\(n_i\)).
  • Current is carried by both electrons and holes: \(I = I_e + I_h\).

4. Extrinsic Semiconductor

Conductivity of pure semiconductors is low. Adding impurities (Doping) increases it significantly. These are Extrinsic Semiconductors.

n-type Semiconductor

Doped with Pentavalent impurity (Valency 5, e.g., As, P, Sb).
- Donates extra electrons.
- Majority carriers: Electrons (\(n_e \gg n_h\)).

p-type Semiconductor

Doped with Trivalent impurity (Valency 3, e.g., B, Al, In).
- Creates vacancies (holes).
- Majority carriers: Holes (\(n_h \gg n_e\)).

5. P-N Junction Formation

When p-type and n-type semiconductors are joined, diffusion of carriers occurs.

  • Holes diffuse from p to n; Electrons from n to p.
  • This creates a Depletion Region devoid of free charges but containing immobile ions.
  • An electric field develops, opposing further diffusion.
Depletion Region
Diffusion of carriers creates a depletion region where recombination occurs.

6. Semiconductor Diode & Rectifier

A p-n junction with metallic contacts is a Diode.

  • Forward Bias: p to (+), n to (-). Depletion width decreases. Current flows easily.
  • Reverse Bias: p to (-), n to (+). Depletion width increases. Current is negligible (only drift current).

Rectifier

Converts AC to DC. Diodes allow current only in one direction.

Rectifier
AC Input (Blue) is converted to pulsating DC Output (Yellow) by the rectifier.
  • Half-Wave Rectifier: Uses 1 diode. Conducts in +ve half cycle only.
  • Full-Wave Rectifier: Uses 2 diodes. Conducts in both cycles.
