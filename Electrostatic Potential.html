1. Introduction & Electrostatic Potential

The electrostatic force is a conservative force, much like the gravitational force. The work done by an external force in moving a charge against an electrostatic field gets stored as potential energy.

Electrostatic Potential Energy

Consider bringing a charge \( q \) from a point R to a point P against the repulsive force of a charge \( Q \). The work done by the external force is stored as potential energy difference:

$$ \Delta U = U_P - U_R = W_{RP} $$
  • The work done depends only on the initial and final positions and is independent of the path taken.
  • The actual value of potential energy is not physically significant; only the difference is significant. We conventionally choose potential energy at infinity to be zero.

Electrostatic Potential (V)

Electrostatic potential is defined as the work done per unit test charge.

$$ V_P = \frac{W_{\infty P}}{q} $$

Definition: Electrostatic potential at a point is the work done by an external force in bringing a unit positive charge (without acceleration) from infinity to that point.

2. Potential due to a Point Charge

Consider a source charge \( Q \) at the origin. To find the potential at a point P distance \( r \) away, we calculate the work done in bringing a unit positive test charge from infinity to P.

$$ V(r) = \frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r} $$
  • \( V \propto 1/r \) (Potential varies inversely with distance).
  • For \( Q > 0 \), potential is positive (Work done against repulsion).
  • For \( Q < 0 \), potential is negative (Work done by attraction).
Graph Contrast: While Electric Field \( E \propto 1/r^2 \), Potential \( V \propto 1/r \).

3. Potential due to an Electric Dipole

An electric dipole consists of two charges \( q \) and \( -q \) separated by a distance \( 2a \). The potential depends on the distance \( r \) and the angle \( \theta \) between the position vector and dipole moment \( \mathbf{p} \).

For large distances (\( r \gg a \)):

$$ V(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{\mathbf{p} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{r}}}{r^2} = \frac{p \cos\theta}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^2} $$

Important Cases:

  • On the Dipole Axis (\( \theta = 0, \pi \)): Potential is maximum. $$ V = \pm \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{p}{r^2} $$
  • On the Equatorial Plane (\( \theta = \pi/2 \)): Potential is zero everywhere on this plane.

4. Potential due to a System of Charges

By the superposition principle, the potential at a point due to a system of charges is the algebraic sum of the potentials due to individual charges.

$$ V = V_1 + V_2 + \dots + V_n = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{q_i}{r_{iP}} $$

5. Equipotential Surfaces

An equipotential surface is a surface where the potential has a constant value at all points.

Key Properties

  1. Shape: For a single point charge, they are concentric spheres centred at the charge. For a uniform electric field, they are planes normal to the field.
  2. Relation to Field: The electric field is always normal to the equipotential surface at every point.
  3. Work Done: No work is required to move a test charge between any two points on an equipotential surface.

Field-Potential Relation

$$ E = -\frac{dV}{dr} $$

The electric field is in the direction where the potential decreases steepest. Its magnitude is the change in potential per unit displacement normal to the surface.

6. Potential Energy of a System of Charges

The potential energy of a system is the work done by an external agency in assembling the charges at their locations, bringing them from infinity.

Two Point Charges

$$ U = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q_1 q_2}{r_{12}} $$

Where \( r_{12} \) is the distance between charges.

7. Electrostatics of Conductors

Conductors contain mobile charge carriers (free electrons). In static equilibrium, they exhibit the following properties:

  1. Inside a conductor, electrostatic field is zero. Free charges distribute themselves to cancel any internal field.
  2. At the surface, electric field must be normal. If there were a tangential component, charges would flow.
  3. The interior can have no excess charge. Any excess charge resides entirely on the surface.
  4. Electrostatic potential is constant throughout the volume and has the same value on the surface.
  5. Electric field at the surface: $$ \mathbf{E} = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0} \hat{\mathbf{n}} $$
  6. Electrostatic Shielding: The electric field inside a cavity of a conductor is always zero, regardless of outside fields.

8. Dielectrics and Polarisation

Dielectrics are non-conducting substances. When placed in an external electric field, they undergo polarisation rather than conduction.

  • Non-polar Molecules: (e.g., \( O_2, H_2 \)) Centers of positive and negative charge coincide. External field induces a dipole moment.
  • Polar Molecules: (e.g., \( HCl, H_2O \)) Have permanent dipole moments. External field tends to align them.

Polarisation (P)

Defined as dipole moment per unit volume. For linear isotropic dielectrics:

$$ \mathbf{P} = \chi_e \mathbf{E} $$

9. Capacitors and Capacitance

A capacitor is a system of two conductors separated by an insulator. It stores electric charge and energy.

Capacitance (C): Defined as the ratio of charge \( Q \) to potential difference \( V \).

$$ C = \frac{Q}{V} $$

The unit is Farad (F). Capacitance depends only on the geometry (shape, size, separation) and the dielectric medium.

Parallel Plate Capacitor

Consists of two parallel plates of area \( A \) separated by distance \( d \).

  • Vacuum between plates: $$ C_0 = \frac{\epsilon_0 A}{d} $$
  • Dielectric between plates: If a dielectric of constant \( K \) fills the space, capacitance increases: $$ C = \frac{K \epsilon_0 A}{d} = K C_0 $$ Where \( K \) is the dielectric constant (\( K > 1 \)).

10. Combination of Capacitors

Capacitors in Series

$$ \frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \dots + \frac{1}{C_n} $$

Capacitors in Parallel

$$ C_{eq} = C_1 + C_2 + \dots + C_n $$

11. Energy Stored in a Capacitor

Work must be done to transfer charge from one plate to another against the potential difference. This work is stored as electrostatic potential energy.

$$ U = \frac{Q^2}{2C} = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{1}{2}QV $$

Energy Density

Energy stored per unit volume in the electric field region:

$$ u = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 E^2 $$
