Alternating Current

Physics • Class 12 • Chapter 7

Detailed Physics Notes

Comprehensive notes based on NCERT Class 12 Physics - Chapter 7

1. Introduction

An electric current whose magnitude changes with time and direction reverses periodically is called Alternating Current (AC).

  • Why AC? AC voltages can be easily stepped up or down using transformers. This reduces energy loss during long-distance transmission.
  • Equation: \( v = v_m \sin(\omega t) \) where \( v_m \) is the peak voltage and \( \omega \) is the angular frequency.

2. AC Voltage Applied to a Resistor

When an AC source is connected to a resistor \( R \), the current is:

$$ i = \frac{v_m}{R} \sin(\omega t) = i_m \sin(\omega t) $$
  • Phase: Current and Voltage are in phase (reach maxima and minima simultaneously).
  • Power: Instantaneous power \( p = i^2 R \). Average power \( P = \frac{1}{2}i_m^2 R \).
  • RMS Value: Root Mean Square current (\( I \)) is defined such that \( P = I^2 R \). $$ I = \frac{i_m}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 0.707 i_m $$ $$ V = \frac{v_m}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 0.707 v_m $$

3. Representation by Phasors

A Phasor is a vector that rotates about the origin with angular speed \( \omega \). Its vertical projection represents the sinusoidally varying quantity (voltage or current).

Figure 7.1 (Animated): A rotating phasor (left) generates a sinusoidal wave (right) over time.

4. AC Voltage Applied to an Inductor

For a pure inductor \( L \), the back emf is \( \varepsilon = -L \frac{di}{dt} \). By Kirchhoff's loop rule:

$$ i = i_m \sin(\omega t - \pi/2) $$
  • Phase: Current lags Voltage by \( \pi/2 \).
  • Inductive Reactance (\( X_L \)): The opposition to current flow. $$ X_L = \omega L $$
  • Power: The average power consumed by a pure inductor is zero.

5. AC Voltage Applied to a Capacitor

For a capacitor \( C \), the voltage is \( v = q/C \). Differentiating charge gives current:

$$ i = i_m \sin(\omega t + \pi/2) $$
  • Phase: Current leads Voltage by \( \pi/2 \).
  • Capacitive Reactance (\( X_C \)): $$ X_C = \frac{1}{\omega C} $$
  • Power: The average power consumed by a pure capacitor is zero.

6. Series LCR Circuit

When Resistor \( R \), Inductor \( L \), and Capacitor \( C \) are connected in series, the impedance \( Z \) is:

$$ Z = \sqrt{R^2 + (X_C - X_L)^2} $$

The phase difference \( \phi \) is given by:

$$ \tan \phi = \frac{X_C - X_L}{R} $$
  • If \( X_C > X_L \): Circuit is capacitive, current leads.
  • If \( X_L > X_C \): Circuit is inductive, current lags.

8. Resonance

In an LCR circuit, resonance occurs when \( X_L = X_C \). At this point, impedance \( Z \) is minimum (\( Z=R \)), and current amplitude is maximum.

$$ \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} $$
Figure 7.3 (Animated): Current amplitude peaks sharply at the resonant frequency \( \omega_0 \).

Used in tuning mechanisms of radios and TVs.

7. Power in AC Circuit

The average power dissipated in an AC circuit depends on the phase difference \( \phi \).

$$ P = V I \cos \phi $$
  • Power Factor (\( \cos \phi \)): \( \cos \phi = R/Z \).
  • Resistive Circuit: \( \phi = 0 \Rightarrow \cos \phi = 1 \), Power is maximum (\( P=VI \)).
  • Purely Inductive/Capacitive: \( \phi = \pi/2 \Rightarrow \cos \phi = 0 \), Power is zero. Current is "Wattless".

9. Transformers

A device used to increase or decrease AC voltage. It works on the principle of Mutual Induction.

Figure 7.4 (Animated): Magnetic flux circulates through the soft-iron core, linking Primary (Amber) and Secondary (Green) coils.

Relation between Voltage (\( V \)), Current (\( I \)), and Turns (\( N \)):

$$ \frac{V_s}{V_p} = \frac{N_s}{N_p} = \frac{I_p}{I_s} $$

Types

  • Step-up: \( N_s > N_p \implies V_s > V_p \). (Increases Voltage, Decreases Current).
  • Step-down: \( N_s < N_p \implies V_s < V_p \). (Decreases Voltage, Increases Current).

Energy Losses

Flux Leakage, Resistance of windings (\( I^2R \)), Eddy Currents, Hysteresis.
