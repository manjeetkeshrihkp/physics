1. Introduction

The nucleus is the central core of an atom where 99.9% of the atom's mass and all of its positive charge are concentrated. Despite containing nearly all the mass, the nucleus is extremely small compared to the atom (\(10^{-12}\) times the volume).

2. Atomic Masses and Composition

Atomic Mass Unit (u): Defined as \(1/12^{th}\) of the mass of a Carbon-12 atom. $$ 1u = 1.660539 \times 10^{-27} \, \text{kg} $$

Composition

The nucleus consists of Nucleons: Protons (positively charged) and Neutrons (neutral).

Figure 13.1 (Animated): A nucleus composed of vibrating Protons (Red) and Neutrons (Gray) held by nuclear force.
  • Atomic Number (\(Z\)): Number of Protons.
  • Neutron Number (\(N\)): Number of Neutrons.
  • Mass Number (\(A\)): Total number of nucleons (\(A = Z + N\)).
  • Notation: \({}_{Z}^{A}X\) (e.g., \({}_{79}^{197}\text{Au}\) denotes Gold).
  • Isotopes: Same \(Z\), different \(N\) (e.g., Deuterium, Tritium).
  • Isobars: Same \(A\), different \(Z\).
  • Isotones: Same \(N\), different \(Z\).

3. Size of the Nucleus

Experiments on alpha-particle scattering revealed that the volume of the nucleus is proportional to its mass number \(A\). Thus, the radius \(R\) is:

$$ R = R_0 A^{1/3} $$

Where \(R_0 = 1.2 \times 10^{-15} \, \text{m} = 1.2 \, \text{fm}\). This implies that Nuclear Density is constant and independent of \(A\) (approx. \(2.3 \times 10^{17} \, \text{kg/m}^3\)).

4. Mass-Energy and Nuclear Binding Energy

Mass-Energy Equivalence

Einstein proved that mass is a form of energy. $$ E = mc^2 $$

Mass Defect (\(\Delta M\))

The mass of a nucleus is always less than the sum of the masses of its constituent protons and neutrons. The difference is the mass defect: $$ \Delta M = [Z m_p + (A-Z) m_n] - M $$

Binding Energy (\(E_b\))

The energy equivalent to the mass defect. It is the energy required to break the nucleus into its constituent nucleons. $$ E_b = \Delta M c^2 $$

5. Nuclear Force

The strong force that binds nucleons together in the nucleus.

  • Short Range: Operates only up to ~3 fm.
  • Strongest Force: Much stronger than Coulomb repulsion between protons.
  • Charge Independent: Force between p-p, n-n, and p-n is approximately the same.
  • Saturation: Nucleons only interact with nearest neighbors.

6. Radioactivity

The spontaneous emission of radiation by unstable nuclei.

Law of Radioactive Decay

The rate of decay is proportional to the number of undecayed nuclei \(N\) present. $$ \frac{dN}{dt} = -\lambda N $$ $$ N(t) = N_0 e^{-\lambda t} $$

Where \(\lambda\) is the decay constant.

Figure 13.3 (Animated): Radioactive sample decaying over time.

Half-Life (\(T_{1/2}\))

Time required for the number of nuclei to reduce to half its initial value. $$ T_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda} = \frac{0.693}{\lambda} $$

Types of Decay

  • Alpha (\(\alpha\)) Decay: Emission of Helium nucleus (\({}_2^4\text{He}\)). Mass number decreases by 4, Atomic number by 2.
  • Beta (\(\beta\)) Decay: Emission of electron (\(\beta^-\)) or positron (\(\beta^+\)). Neutrons convert to protons or vice versa.
  • Gamma (\(\gamma\)) Decay: Emission of high-energy photons from an excited nucleus. No change in \(A\) or \(Z\).

7. Nuclear Energy

Nuclear Fission

A heavy nucleus (like Uranium-235) splits into two lighter nuclei when bombarded by a neutron, releasing massive energy and more neutrons.

$$ {}_0^1\text{n} + {}_{92}^{235}\text{U} \rightarrow {}_{56}^{144}\text{Ba} + {}_{36}^{89}\text{Kr} + 3 {}_0^1\text{n} + \text{Energy} $$
Figure 13.4 (Animated): A neutron strikes a Uranium nucleus, causing it to split into fragments and release energy.

Nuclear Fusion

Two light nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus, releasing energy. This is the source of energy in stars (e.g., the Sun). $$ {}_1^1\text{H} + {}_1^1\text{H} \rightarrow {}_1^2\text{H} + e^+ + \nu + \text{Energy} $$

Fusion requires extremely high temperatures (Thermonuclear Fusion) to overcome Coulomb repulsion.
