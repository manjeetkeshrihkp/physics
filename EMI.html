1. Introduction & Faraday's Experiments

Electromagnetic Induction is the phenomenon where electric currents are generated by varying magnetic fields. This was discovered by Michael Faraday and Joseph Henry around 1830.

Faraday's Experiments

Figure 6.1: Moving a magnet towards a coil induces a momentary current. The galvanometer deflects only during relative motion.
  • Experiment 1 (Motion): When a bar magnet is pushed towards a coil, the galvanometer deflects. It deflects in the opposite direction when the magnet is pulled away.
  • Experiment 2 (Current Loop): Replacing the magnet with a current-carrying coil works too. Moving the current-carrying coil induces current in the secondary coil.
  • Experiment 3 (Changing Current): Even without motion, if the current in the primary coil changes (e.g., by tapping a key), a momentary current is induced in the secondary coil.

2. Magnetic Flux

Magnetic flux (\(\Phi_B\)) is proportional to the number of magnetic field lines crossing a surface.

$$ \Phi_B = \mathbf{B} \cdot \mathbf{A} = BA \cos \theta $$
  • \(\theta\) is the angle between the magnetic field \(\mathbf{B}\) and the area vector \(\mathbf{A}\).
  • Unit: Weber (Wb) or Tesla-meter squared (\(T m^2\)).

3. Faraday's Law of Induction

The Law: The magnitude of the induced emf (\(\varepsilon\)) in a circuit is equal to the time rate of change of magnetic flux through the circuit.

$$ \varepsilon = - \frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} $$

For a coil with \(N\) turns, the flux change is associated with each turn, so:

$$ \varepsilon = - N \frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} $$

The negative sign indicates the direction of the induced emf (Lenz's Law).

4. Lenz's Law

Statement: The polarity of induced emf is such that it tends to produce a current which opposes the change in magnetic flux that produced it.

This law is a consequence of the **Conservation of Energy**. Work must be done against this repulsive force to move the magnet, which is converted into electrical energy.

5. Motional Electromotive Force

When a conductor moves in a magnetic field, the charge carriers experience a Lorentz force, causing charge separation and inducing an emf.

6. Inductance

Flux linkage (\(N\Phi\)) is proportional to current (\(I\)). The constant of proportionality is called Inductance.

Mutual Inductance

When current changes in one coil, it induces emf in a neighboring coil.

$$ N_2 \Phi_2 = M_{21} I_1 $$ $$ \varepsilon_2 = -M_{21} \frac{dI_1}{dt} $$

Reciprocity Theorem: For two coils, the mutual inductance is the same regardless of which carries the current:

$$ M_{12} = M_{21} = M $$

Self-Inductance

The phenomenon where a changing current in a coil induces a back emf in the same coil.

$$ \varepsilon = -L \frac{dI}{dt} $$

For a long solenoid: \( L = \mu_0 n^2 A l \). Energy stored in an inductor: \( U = \frac{1}{2} L I^2 \).

7. AC Generator

An AC generator converts mechanical energy into electrical energy. It works on the principle of electromagnetic induction by rotating a coil in a magnetic field.

Figure 6.5: As the coil rotates, the magnetic flux through it changes continuously, generating an Alternating Current (AC).

When the coil is rotated with angular speed \(\omega\), the angle is \(\theta = \omega t\). The magnetic flux is:

$$ \Phi_B = B A \cos(\omega t) $$

From Faraday's law, the induced emf is:

$$ \varepsilon = -N \frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = -NBA \frac{d}{dt}(\cos \omega t) = NBA \omega \sin(\omega t) $$

Where \(\varepsilon_0 = NBA\omega\) is the maximum emf.
