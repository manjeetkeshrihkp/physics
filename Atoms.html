1. Introduction

J.J. Thomson (1898) proposed the first model of the atom, often called the plum pudding model. He suggested that the positive charge is uniformly distributed throughout the atom, and electrons are embedded in it like seeds in a watermelon. This model failed to explain the results of alpha-particle scattering experiments.

2. Rutherford's Nuclear Model

Ernest Rutherford, along with Hans Geiger and Ernest Marsden, performed the famous \(\alpha\)-particle scattering experiment.

The Experiment

A beam of high-energy \(\alpha\)-particles (He\(^{2+}\)) was directed at a thin gold foil.

Trajectories of alpha particles. Most pass straight through (ap1), some deflect (ap2), and very few bounce back (ap3).

Observations & Conclusions

  • Observation: Most particles passed straight through.
    Conclusion: Most of the space inside the atom is empty.
  • Observation: Some were deflected by large angles.
    Conclusion: Positive charge is concentrated in a tiny central core called the Nucleus.
  • Observation: A very small fraction (1 in 8000) rebounded (\(180^\circ\)).
    Conclusion: The nucleus contains nearly all the mass of the atom.

Impact Parameter (\(b\))

The perpendicular distance of the velocity vector of the \(\alpha\)-particle from the centre of the nucleus when it is far away.

  • If \(b\) is large, scattering angle \(\theta\) is small.
  • If \(b \approx 0\) (head-on collision), \(\theta \approx 180^\circ\).

3. Atomic Spectra

When an atomic gas or vapor is excited at low pressure, the emitted radiation has a spectrum containing certain specific wavelengths only. This is called a Line Spectrum.

Spectral Series of Hydrogen

The frequencies of light emitted by hydrogen follow a pattern given by the Rydberg formula:

$$ \frac{1}{\lambda} = R \left( \frac{1}{n_f^2} - \frac{1}{n_i^2} \right) $$

Where \(R = 1.097 \times 10^7 \, \text{m}^{-1}\) is the Rydberg constant.

Series Region Formula (\(n_f, n_i\))
Lyman Ultraviolet \(n_f=1, n_i=2,3...\)
Balmer Visible \(n_f=2, n_i=3,4...\)
Paschen Infrared \(n_f=3, n_i=4,5...\)

4. Bohr Model of the Hydrogen Atom

Niels Bohr modified Rutherford's model by applying quantum ideas.

Bohr's Postulates

  1. Stationary Orbits: Electrons revolve in stable orbits without radiating energy.
  2. Quantization Condition: The angular momentum \(L\) of the electron is an integral multiple of \(h/2\pi\).
    $$ L = mvr = \frac{nh}{2\pi} \quad (n=1, 2, 3...) $$
  3. Frequency Condition: Energy is emitted only when an electron jumps from a higher orbit (\(E_i\)) to a lower orbit (\(E_f\)).
    $$ h\nu = E_i - E_f $$
Electron jumping from higher to lower orbits emits a photon (blue flash).

Radii of Bohr Orbits

The radius of the \(n\)-th orbit is given by:

$$ r_n = \frac{n^2 h^2 \epsilon_0}{\pi m e^2} $$

For hydrogen (\(n=1\)), the Bohr radius \(a_0 = 0.529 \text{ \AA}\). Thus, \(r_n = n^2 a_0\).

5. Energy Levels

The total energy of the electron in the \(n\)-th orbit is:

$$ E_n = -\frac{me^4}{8\epsilon_0^2 h^2} \left( \frac{1}{n^2} \right) \approx -\frac{13.6}{n^2} \, \text{eV} $$
  • Ground State (\(n=1\)): \(E_1 = -13.6\) eV. This is the most stable state.
  • Excited States: \(E_2 = -3.4\) eV, \(E_3 = -1.51\) eV, etc.
  • Ionization Energy: The energy required to remove the electron from the ground state to infinity (\(n=\infty, E=0\)) is \(13.6\) eV.

6. De Broglie's Explanation of Quantization

Louis de Broglie explained Bohr's second postulate (angular momentum quantization) using the wave nature of electrons.

For an electron wave to be stable in an orbit, the circumference of the orbit must act as a standing wave. This means the circumference must be an integral multiple of the wavelength.

A standing electron wave fitting perfectly into the orbit (\(2\pi r = n\lambda\)).
$$ 2\pi r = n\lambda $$

Since \(\lambda = h/mv\) (De Broglie relation):

$$ 2\pi r = n \frac{h}{mv} \implies mvr = \frac{nh}{2\pi} $$

This derivation successfully connected the wave nature of matter with the quantization of angular momentum.
