Dual Nature of Radiation & Matter

Physics • Class 12 • Chapter 11

Detailed Physics Notes

Comprehensive notes based on NCERT Class 12 Physics - Chapter 11

1. Introduction

Wave-Particle Duality: Radiation and matter exhibit both wave-like and particle-like properties.

  • Electron Emission: Metals have free electrons. The minimum energy required to pull an electron out of the metal surface is called the Work Function (\(\phi_0\)).
  • Types of Emission:
    • Thermionic Emission: By heating.
    • Field Emission: By applying a strong electric field.
    • Photoelectric Emission: By illuminating with light of suitable frequency.

2. Photoelectric Effect

The emission of electrons from a metal surface when light of suitable frequency falls on it is called the Photoelectric Effect. The emitted electrons are called photoelectrons.

Incident light (amber) strikes the metal plate, causing the instantaneous emission of electrons (green) towards the collector.

Experimental Observations

  • Intensity: Photocurrent is directly proportional to the intensity of incident light.
  • Potential: As the positive potential of the collector increases, current reaches a maximum called Saturation Current. If the potential is made negative, the current stops at a specific value called Stopping Potential (\(V_0\)).
  • Frequency: Stopping potential varies linearly with the frequency of incident radiation. There exists a minimum frequency below which no emission occurs, called Threshold Frequency (\(\nu_0\)).
  • Instantaneous Process: Emission occurs within \(10^{-9}\) s.

Failure of Wave Theory: The wave theory could not explain the instantaneous emission or the existence of a threshold frequency, as it predicted energy absorption would be continuous and take time.

3. Einstein's Photoelectric Equation

Albert Einstein (1905) explained the effect by proposing that light consists of discrete packets of energy called Quanta or Photons. The energy of each photon is \(E = h\nu\).

$$ K_{max} = h\nu - \phi_0 $$ $$ eV_0 = h\nu - \phi_0 $$
Stopping Potential (\(V_0\))
Frequency (\(\nu\))
\(\nu_0\)

This equation explains all observations:
- \(K_{max}\) depends on \(\nu\), not intensity.
- Emission happens only if \(h\nu > \phi_0\) (explaining threshold frequency).
- Increasing intensity increases the number of photons, hence number of electrons (current), but not their energy.

4. Particle Nature of Light: The Photon

Light interacts with matter as if it is made of particles called photons.

  • Energy: \(E = h\nu = hc/\lambda\).
  • Momentum: \(p = h\nu/c = h/\lambda\).
  • Photons are electrically neutral.
  • In photon-particle collisions, total energy and momentum are conserved.
  • Photon mass is zero (rest mass).

5. Wave Nature of Matter

De Broglie Hypothesis (1924): Just as radiation has a dual nature, matter (particles like electrons) also exhibits wave-like properties.

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv} $$

This is the De Broglie Wavelength.

A moving particle (electron) has an associated wave of wavelength \(\lambda = h/p\).

De Broglie Wavelength of an Electron

For an electron accelerated through a potential \(V\):

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2mK}} = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2meV}} $$ $$ \lambda \approx \frac{1.227}{\sqrt{V}} \, \text{nm} $$

6. Davisson and Germer Experiment

This experiment provided the first experimental proof of the wave nature of electrons.

  • A beam of electrons was scattered by a Nickel crystal.
  • A strong peak in intensity was observed at a scattering angle of \(50^\circ\) for an accelerating voltage of 54 V.
  • This peak is due to the constructive interference of electron waves diffracted by the crystal lattice.
Electron beam scattering off a crystal lattice. The diffraction peak confirms wave behavior.

The wavelength calculated from diffraction data matched the theoretical de Broglie wavelength (\(\lambda \approx 0.167\) nm), confirming the hypothesis.
